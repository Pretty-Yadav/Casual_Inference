Average Treatment Effect (ATE)

Our Result:

0.13 standard deviations

Increasing study hours leads to this much improvement in student performance

What is the Average Treatment Effect?

The Average Treatment Effect (ATE) is the average difference in student performance between those who studied more (treated group) and those who studied less (control group), after matching them on similar characteristics.

In simple terms: "On average, how much better do students perform when they study more?"

How to Interpret 0.13

0.13 standard deviations means the treated group's performance is 0.13 SDs above the control group.

Effect Size Interpretation Our Result
~0.1 - 0.2 Small effect ✓ 0.13 (our result)
~0.3 - 0.5 Medium effect
0.5+ Large effect

What Does This Mean Practically?

  • Positive Impact: Studying more genuinely improves performance—not just random variation
  • Modest Size: The effect is small but meaningful. It's not life-changing, but it matters
  • Real-World Significance: If a student starts with average performance, extra study would move them up by a small but noticeable amount
  • Causal: This is a causal effect (not just correlation) because we used propensity score matching to control for confounding

Why This Matters

Even small causal effects are valuable for policy and practice. If we know that every extra hour of study causally improves performance by a measurable amount, we have evidence to recommend increased study as an intervention.
